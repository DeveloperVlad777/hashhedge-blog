CROSSING THE US BORDER WITH CARTELS
Making $10,000–$15,000 a Month Trading at Age 22
12 min read | July 18, 2026
Mardon is 22 years old. Three years ago, he journeyed from Uzbekistan to the US via Istanbul and Mexico. He crossed the border with the help of cartels: on a homemade raft, hidden under a truck tarp, and running under the glare of border patrol searchlights. Once in the States, he took any job he could find: handing out flyers, delivering food, making deliveries on a scooter, and packaging illicit substances. Just 8 months ago, Mardon got into trading. Today, it's his sole source of income: he makes $10,000–$15,000 a month, fully supporting himself, his girlfriend, and his mother. Mardon currently lives in Brooklyn and has big plans: he wants to launch a trading blog, is saving up for a road trip across America, and is reapplying for Wall Street internships.

Moving and $10,000 to the Cartels
The family decided to move to America in just three days. As soon as they got in touch with the right people to organize the border crossing, they made an initial $10,000 payment for Mardon. The countdown began.
"The next day, they told me I was flying to Turkey. I rushed to say goodbye to my friends and left. I didn't really even understand what was happening."
Istanbul, the Buffer Zone, and a Mother's Deportation
Mardon ended up in Istanbul alone. He stayed the night in a hotel, expecting his mother to fly in the next day. However, she was denied entry into Turkey due to a prior deportation from the country. The layout of Istanbul Airport includes a technical buffer zone – a space "between" borders where you can be without formally entering Turkey. His mother bought a new ticket and flew directly into this zone. That's where they met up, spending the night right there in the airport. Next came a flight through Guatemala or Nicaragua (Mardon doesn't recall the exact geography of this stage) and a brief rest by a pool overlooking the jungle.
"I thought to myself, if the whole journey is like this, I'm down. But that wasn't the case."
A Tire Raft, a Tarped Truck, and the Border Fence
Next was the trek through Mexico: 6-to-12-hour bus rides with almost no rest stops, spending just a couple of hours sleeping in strangers' houses between legs of the trip. They were ordered to hide their money. Along the way, both police and cartels stopped travelers to check for valuables. If they found cash, they would hold the person until relatives paid a ransom. Mardon and his mother hid their money inside a baby powder bottle.
They crossed one of the rivers – effectively a large lake – on a raft made of truck tires and wooden planks, 5 to 6 people at a time. "Just don't fall in," the guides warned, without explaining why. As it turned out, the water was full of snakes and crocodiles. A separate boat ferried cars across. Closer to the border, a group of about 40 people was packed into a truck and covered with a black tarp like cargo. At one point, police with flashing lights pulled them over. The driver seemingly settled it on the spot, and they kept moving a couple of minutes later.
"They told us we were being transported along with some bad things," Mardon recalls.
The final dash to the border was the most terrifying moment: running about a kilometer at night, past a construction site, under the searchlights and laser sights of border guards. Mardon carried two massive backpacks so his mother wouldn't have to carry the weight.
"If you don't cross the border in time, you get detained and sent back to your country. We had to make it."
They crossed the actual border at a section with barbed wire covered by a blanket. An eight-year-old girl in the group fell onto the wire while crossing; a man who rushed to save her was also cut up, but both were taken for medical treatment and no one was seriously hurt. Contrary to Mardon's fears, there was no gunfire. They crossed the border successfully.
First Months in the US: Waiter, Scooter, and Queens
After crossing, Mardon was detained and placed in a migrant hotel. Stays were limited to 2 days, but he managed to negotiate 5. His mother was held separately and for a much longer time. With no connections in the country except for one Uzbek family, Mardon asked to stay with them. Their son found him his first job – working as a waiter at Jewish weddings in New York.
Later, he worked as a food delivery courier on a scooter. The scooter belonged to someone else and broke down constantly. Once in Manhattan, the chain snapped, and Mardon had to push it on foot through the rain for 3 hours until a random bystander helped him wheel it home.
While in the detention center, his mother used other detainees to find a sponsor with a green card. He helped get her released in a couple of days, whereas others were demanding $2,500 for the same assistance. The Uzbek community also stepped up, offering his mother free food and housing.
Los Angeles: Capsule Hostel and Packaging Illicit Substances
On a friend's advice, the family moved to Los Angeles, where there were more jobs. They arrived with just $500 in their pockets. A standard temporary homeless shelter didn't appeal to them based on descriptions of the conditions, but a capsule mini-hostel (essentially bunks built into the wall) cost $650 a month per person. The owners, who were originally from Kazakhstan, made an exception: his mother cleaned the hostel to cover rent, and Mardon promised to pay the rest as soon as he started earning.
They found their first job through Facebook and Telegram groups for migrants: packaging illicit substances at a facility. Mardon understood it to be a mix of medical and non-medical distribution. While there, he was offered a chance to make quick cash through insurance fraud – intentionally getting into a car accident for $3,000 from the organizers. He declined after learning it would spike his own insurance rates. He had to leave that job because the law required workers to be over 21, and Mardon was only 19 at the time. Instead, he went to hand out advertising flyers door-to-door in Santa Monica. A tracking app monitored his movement and issued a $100 fine for stopping. He walked 40 to 50 kilometers a day for $680 a week.
"The first few weeks were incredibly brutal. Then my body adapted, and I even started going to the gym."

From Binary Options to Trading Mentor
Mardon first encountered trading back in Uzbekistan when he was 13. He deposited about $50 in savings into a binary options platform, intending to spend the profits on in-game purchases. He blew the money quickly and forgot about it for years. Once in the US, he remembered the concept and returned to the same platform. He started learning from YouTube videos and traded 5-second candles. He turned a $100 deposit into $700, but immediately fell into a state he describes as "gambling addiction": "I tried to win back my money after the first losses. The stakes grew, and I ended up blowing the entire deposit."
The turning point was another YouTube channel. The creator warned that 1-minute and 5-second charts aren't trading – they are roulette. Trading needs to happen on timeframes ranging from 15 minutes to several hours. Mardon adopted this approach, and his trading results became more stable.
