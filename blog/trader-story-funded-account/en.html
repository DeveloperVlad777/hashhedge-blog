Denis put his last $2,000 into a financial pyramid scheme — and that turned out to be just the beginning. What followed: BTC at $320, ETH at $4, an accident after which doctors wouldn't promise he'd ever walk again, gravel, halva and beat-up Lada cars, a trust account blown to zero… On Hash Hedge he started his trading journey with a failure: the first challenge was gone in days, and in the second he barely held on at the edge (drawdown reached 7% against an 8% limit). This story confirms the market's core rule: the road to steady profit is winding, and holding on to capital is always harder than earning it.
Pyramid Schemes, First BTC and ETH at $4
Denis invested the first serious money he had saved — from selling gravel wholesale — into the financial pyramid Helix in 2014. He made a profit. Decided the scheme worked and invested again — this time in a different pyramid. That time he lost everything. It was through the Helix myth that he first heard about BTC: the organisers claimed to invest part of the funds into cryptocurrency. Curiosity took over — he started researching on his own and bought his first coins through the Bestchange exchange.
"In December 2015 I bought 10 BTC at $320. The price hit $540 — I thought: 'Wow, those are Xs, two pizzas please, I don't want to lose money.' I sold everything and locked in the profit."
Then came Ethereum, which Denis heard about through yet another pyramid, F-Trade. He bought at $4, $6 and $8, and sold at $30.
"Do I regret it? What's the point of regret. FOMO exists — I'm not a robot. We talk about it with friends, we laugh, my brother laughs at me — that's fine, everything's okay."
The Accident After Which the Doctor Only Promised a Wheelchair
In 2012, at age 20, Denis was in a serious car accident: he was a passenger, the car crashed at night in the rain. One of his friends died on the spot; Denis himself fell into a coma. The result — paralysis of the left side of his body, partial memory loss, a long recovery.
"The doctor treating me said: not everyone will be in a wheelchair, but if he walks at all, only with a cane. I told him: 'I'll be walking now.' He was in shock when he saw me half a year after I was discharged — he nearly cried."
By 2020 Denis had run a half-marathon. This episode is not just a biographical detail — it explains why financial losses, even large ones, didn't derail him the way they might derail someone else.
"All problems aren't problems — they're tasks that need to be solved. I just really wanted to live, and I still do."
Gravel, Halva and 8 Rental Cars: Life Hustling
Even before trading and crypto, Denis had tried pretty much everything that could be resold or rented out: game discs back when the internet barely existed, phones through classified ads (including dropshipping iPhones from the US with a friend), gravel sold directly to buyers bypassing the quarry, driving a taxi, renting out cars (at his peak he had 8), delivering halva to shops in a minivan and fixing up beat-up Lada cars for resale.
"Life is experience. Nothing in life happens for no reason — you have to remember that and keep it in mind."
He also recalls a story from his vocational college days: after receiving his scholarship, he quickly relaxed, stopped studying and lost the payments — then deliberately pulled his grades up and got the scholarship back in his final year. A simple lesson that, by his account, came back to him many years later in trading: discipline and consistency work, while relaxation costs money.
Scalping, Blown Capital and a Friend's Money
Having made money on gravel, Denis first put his savings back into pyramid schemes again (same result — lost everything), then into trading. He tried scalping, quickly scaled up positions on a winning streak, and lost just as quickly.
"I don't like long learning curves and dragging things out. Straight to business, straight to practice. Maybe that's why I lost so much money."
The most painful loss came when he took a substantial sum from a friend into a trust account and blew it. They split the risks and the loss fifty-fifty; Denis has already paid back half of the debt.
During the bear market of 2018–2019 he sold all his crypto assets at the bottom, convinced the market had "died for good," and stepped away from the space entirely for a couple of years (having also dipped into ICOs in 2017). He returned in 2020 through DeFi and Telegram airdrop farming: some drops (Notcoin, Hamster Combat) paid off, projects like Taiko, Scroll, Zora and Linea yielded almost nothing. The exception was StarkNet, which according to Denis turned out to be the only airdrop that truly covered the time and gas fees spent.

How Denis Discovered Hash Hedge and Blew His First Challenge
Denis learned about prop trading from an interview with trader and influencer Nikita Anufriev, whose videos he had been following for a long time.
"He's saying: guys, there's Hash Hedge, a prop firm, they give you capital to manage. My first thought: where's the catch here? But Nikita is the number-one influencer — he's hardly going to risk his reputation for pennies. Decided to try — took the minimum account."
Getting to grips with the rules didn't happen right away, and the first account was gone in a couple of days — he hit the daily loss limit because he didn't close a trade that had a non-negative but unrealised result, figuring that since the loss wasn't "realised," nothing bad would happen.
"I'm a direct person: if something's unclear, I write to support straight away instead of reading manuals for hours. I wrote, they explained everything — I opened a new account and kept trading. That account is still alive."
Second Challenge: 7 of 8% Drawdown and the Comeback
Denis almost lost the second account too: the drawdown reached nearly 7% against the maximum allowed 8%. Buying a new challenge would have been cheap, but on principle he didn't want to — he decided to rescue this one.
To do that he cut his risk per trade from 1% to 0.5% of the deposit, keeping the risk/reward ratio at 1:3, and started trading "little by little," especially during the difficult market conditions at the end of 2025.
In early June 2025 his favourite trend-breakout setup triggered — the drawdown was recovered, the account turned positive, and Denis made his first withdrawal.
October 10: Market Crash, Private Channel and the Funded Account
The pivotal moment of the story is October 10, 2025 — the day the market dropped sharply on the back of new Trump tariffs and liquidity issues on Binance. Denis was already a member of Nikita Anufriev's free private community at that point (access is granted via a Bybit referral link and a deposit of $2,000 or more).
"Nikita wrote in the chat: guys, there's going to be a short, we're going short. I opened the chart, looked myself — it really looked like a short, I could see the weakness. Went short, went to sleep. Woke up — take profit hit, account went funded."
Since then Denis has traded primarily BTC, ETH and SOL, citing the highest liquidity and the absence of the strict limits typical of altcoins. Looking ahead, he wants to narrow his focus down to Bitcoin alone, calling it "the S&P 500 of the crypto market" — the asset that sets the mood for everything else.
The Main Lesson: Earning Isn't the Problem — Keeping Capital Is
Asked what the biggest challenge was across all these years, Denis answers without hesitation:
"Earning money was never a problem for me. The problem is keeping it. Even Alexander Gerchik says in his lessons: the market will always give you a chance to earn — the question is whether you can avoid losing it."
His advice to those just starting to trade on prop accounts is simple and straightforward:
—
learn one strategy — that's enough, don't spread yourself thin;
—
backtest it and practice on a demo account or paper trades;
—
follow your risk management strictly and never move your stop-loss "for luck";
—
don't chase quick money — trading, in Denis's words, is boring, methodical work, not a source of easy income;
—
be prepared for the fact that reaching consistent results can take six months or a year.
Key Takeaways
1
The path is rarely straight.
From pyramid schemes and gravel to a funded account on Hash Hedge, Denis went through more than ten years of trial, error and varied income streams. The experience of losses — in pyramids, in scalping, managing a friend's money — is not an exception to the success story; it's an essential part of it.
2
A blown challenge is not the end — it's a test run.
Denis lost the first account on Hash Hedge because he didn't know the rules, not because he lacked skill. Once he understood the risk management and reached out to support, he opened a new account and took it all the way to funded status.
3
Cutting risk saved the deposit.
By reducing risk per trade from 1% to 0.5% and keeping the risk/reward ratio at 1:3, Denis pulled the account back from nearly 7% in the red and turned it positive.
4
Discipline works — he learned that back in vocational college.
The mechanism is the same whether it's a scholarship at 17 or an account on Hash Hedge: act on a clear plan and results come; relax and you lose what you already earned.
